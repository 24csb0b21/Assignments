Lotus Temple

New Delhi, India

History of the Lotus Temple

The Lotus Temple, also known as the Bahá'í House of Worship, is a stunning architectural marvel located in New Delhi, India. It is dedicated to the Bahá'í Faith and serves as a place for people of all religions to come together for prayer and meditation.

The temple's unique lotus flower design was conceived by Iranian architect Fariborz Sahba and completed in 1986. The structure consists of 27 free-standing marble-clad petals arranged in a way that resembles a blooming lotus flower. It is surrounded by lush gardens and a serene atmosphere that makes it a peaceful retreat for visitors.

Architectural Significance

The Lotus Temple is not only an iconic landmark of New Delhi but also an important symbol of unity and peace. Its architectural design represents the Bahá'í concept of oneness of humanity, and its open design invites people of all faiths to come together in prayer and reflection. The temple's nine sides represent the nine major world religions, and its open central hall can accommodate more than 2,500 people.

Conclusion

The Lotus Temple is a testament to the ideals of love, unity, and peace, and its beauty attracts visitors from around the world. It stands as a beacon of hope for humanity, offering a space where all can come together, regardless of their background or beliefs.
